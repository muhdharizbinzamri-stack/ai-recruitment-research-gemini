Investigating the Role of AI and Humans Across Recruitment Stages

A Case Study in Malaysia examining how technical efficiency and human social judgment complement one another in modern talent acquisition.

Abstract

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is fundamentally transforming human resource management by vastly improving the efficiency, speed, and data processing capabilities of hiring decisions. However, the exact roles of AI-based versus human-based recruitment methods across specific, sequential recruitment stages remain heavily debated and unclear, particularly within the emerging digital landscape of Malaysia.

While AI can support faster and highly systematic recruitment processes by eliminating repetitive tasks and sorting massive datasets, human judgment remains indispensable. Hiring decisions inherently require emotional intelligence, ethical responsibility, cultural contextualization, and an in-depth assessment of candidate fit—qualities machines currently cannot replicate.

This study aims to bridge this gap by investigating and measuring the necessary level of involvement for both AI and human methods across six key recruitment stages: (1) identifying needs/job analysis, (2) sourcing and attraction, (3) screening and shortlisting, (4) assessment, (5) interview, and (6) induction and onboarding. Utilizing a quantitative, cross-sectional design underpinned by Sociotechnical Systems (STS) Theory and Human-AI Complementarity, data will be collected from a minimum of 100 HR practitioners and hiring managers across Malaysia. Ultimately, this research provides practical guidance for organizations to systematically allocate AI and human involvement, thereby improving recruitment efficiency, maintaining ethical practices, and fostering candidate trust.

The Problem

Most existing literature discusses AI recruitment in broad, generalized terms, focusing heavily on theoretical benefits or ethical risks (like algorithmic bias). However, there is a severe lack of operational guidance on how these methods should be integrated into specific, day-to-day recruitment stages.

Without a clear roadmap, Malaysian organizations risk making structural errors: they may overuse AI in stages that critically require human judgment (such as cultural fit interviews), or they may underuse AI in transactional stages where automation could vastly improve efficiency (like resume screening). This imbalance leads to unfair hiring decisions, reduced transparency, and a loss of candidate trust.

Objectives & Scope

Research Objectives

  • To investigate the role of AI-based recruitment methods across specific recruitment stages in Malaysia.
  • To investigate the role of human-based recruitment methods across specific recruitment stages in Malaysia.

Scope of Study

The population focuses strictly on individuals currently involved in recruitment and selection activities within Malaysia (HR practitioners, recruiters, hiring managers). The organizations must have their own internal HR department to ensure they manage recruitment directly with structured, systematic stages rather than fully outsourced operations.

Proposed Hypotheses Across 6 Stages

Stage 1: Job Analysis

H1: Human-based recruitment methods have a higher level of involvement than AI-based methods in identifying needs and job analysis.

Stage 2: Sourcing

H2: AI-based recruitment methods have a higher level of involvement than human-based methods in sourcing and attraction.

Stage 3: Screening

H3: AI-based recruitment methods have a higher level of involvement than human-based methods in screening and shortlisting.

Stage 4: Assessment

H4: AI-based recruitment methods have a higher level of involvement than human-based methods in the assessment stage.

Stage 5: Interview

H5: Human-based recruitment methods have a higher level of involvement than AI-based methods in the interview stage.

Stage 6: Onboarding

H6: Human-based recruitment methods have a higher level of involvement than AI-based methods in induction and onboarding.

Literature Review & Conceptual Framework

Underpinning Theories

Sociotechnical Systems (STS) Theory

Originally developed to study the psychological impacts of mechanization, STS theory posits that technological changes do not happen in isolation. The technical subsystem (algorithms, data) and the social subsystem (human values, culture) must be designed together. In Malaysia, where culture heavily relies on personal chemistry in hiring, AI must be integrated with social systems rather than treated as a standalone fix.

Human-AI Complementarity

This theory focuses on achieving Complementary Team Performance (CTP), where humans and AI are functionally different but highly effective when combined based on capability asymmetries. AI excels at rapid pattern detection in large datasets (sourcing/screening), while humans excel at causal interpretation, intuition, and establishing social trust (interviews/onboarding).

Variables of Study

  • Independent Variable 1 Recruitment Methods

    Comparing AI-based systems (machine learning, NLP) against traditional Human-based methods (professional judgment, interaction).

  • Independent Variable 2 The 6 Recruitment Stages

    Job Analysis, Sourcing & Attraction, Screening & Shortlisting, Assessment, Interview, and Induction & Onboarding.

  • Dependent Variable Level of Involvement

    The measured extent of participation (from Very Low to Very High) required by either AI or Humans at each specific stage.

Research Methodology

Research Design

Quantitative, cross-sectional design testing comparison-based hypotheses. Data is collected at a single point in time to capture current organizational practices efficiently.

Population & Sampling

Minimum 100 HR practitioners and hiring managers in Malaysia. Utilizing non-probability techniques: purposive, convenience, and snowball sampling via LinkedIn and email.

Measurement

Self-administered online questionnaire utilizing a 5-point involvement scale ranging from (1) Very Low Involvement to (5) Very High Involvement.

Data Analysis

IBM SPSS Statistics for demographic analysis, reliability, and mean comparisons. SmartPLS will be deployed for rigorous measurement model validation via PLS-SEM.
