Investigating the Role of AI and Human Across Recruitment Stages: A Case Study in Malaysia

Abstract

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is increasingly transforming recruitment by improving efficiency, speed and data processing in hiring decisions. However, the roles of AI-based and human-based recruitment methods across specific recruitment stages remain unclear, especially in Malaysia where AI adoption in recruitment is still emerging. Although AI can support faster and more systematic recruitment processes, human judgment is still important in hiring decisions that require emotional intelligence, personal understanding and assessment of candidate fit. Most existing literature discusses AI recruitment in general terms, leaving a research gap on how AI and human roles should be integrated across specific recruitment stages.

Therefore, this study will investigate the roles of AI-based and human-based recruitment methods by measuring their level of involvement across six key recruitment stages in Malaysia: identifying needs or job analysis, sourcing and attraction, screening and shortlisting, assessment, interview and induction and onboarding. This study will use a quantitative, cross-sectional research design underpinned by Sociotechnical Systems (STS) Theory and Human-AI Complementarity. Data will be collected from at least 100 human resource practitioners, recruiters and hiring managers in Malaysia who are currently involved in hiring, recruitment or talent acquisition activities using purposive, convenience and snowball sampling methods. The data will be analysed using IBM SPSS Statistics for demographic analysis, mean comparison analysis and hypothesis testing, while SmartPLS will be used to validate the measurement model. The expected findings will identify which recruitment stages are more suitable for AI-based methods and which stages are more suitable for human-based methods. This study is expected to provide practical guidance for Malaysian organizations to allocate AI and human involvement more systematically while improving recruitment efficiency, ethical practice and candidate trust.

1.0 Introduction

1.1 Background of Study

Artificial intelligence (AI) is becoming more important in recruitment and selection as organizations want to make the hiring process faster, more proper and more efficient. Technology strongly influences parts of the recruitment life cycle, including attraction, screening, selection and onboarding. Therefore, this study focuses on AI-based and human-based recruitment methods and recruitment stages as the independent variables, while the dependent variable is the level of AI and human involvement across recruitment stages.

AI-based recruitment methods can help organizations manage recruitment activity more efficiently. Recruiters value efficiency, time saving and automation as some of the main benefits of using AI in recruitment. AI can improve recruitment quality, increase efficiency and reduce repetitive work. This shows that AI may be useful in recruitment stages that involve large amount of applicant data, repetitive tasks and faster processing. However, AI-based recruitment methods also have weakness and limitations. AI recruitment systems may produce unfair results if the data or system design contains bias. Algorithmic bias in AI-enabled recruitment can lead to discriminatory hiring practices based on gender, race, colour and personality traits. AI recruitment creates ethical concerns because AI tools are used in important hiring decisions, such as writing job advertisements, screening resumes and analysing video interviews.

Human-based recruitment methods remain important because recruitment decision do not only depend on technical matching or automated screening. Lack of human judgement is one of the main disadvantages of AI use in recruitment. AI support in later selection stages, such as telephone or online interview, may reduce candidate's opportunity to perform well and increase feeling of discomfort. At the same time, human recruiters may be influence by personal judgement, preference, unconscious bias, or subjective impressions during the recruitment process. Collaboration between recruiters and AI is important because traditional recruitment methods may involve human prejudice, while AI can help improve recruitment efficiency and support decision making. However, AI should not fully replace recruiters because recruitment decisions still involve human values and ethical responsibility.

1.2 Problem Statement

Even though AI is increasingly use in recruitment and selection, the level of AI and human involvement across different recruitment stages remains unclear. The problem is that both AI-based and human-based recruitment methods have strength and weakness, but existing studies still do not clearly explain how both methods should be applied across specific recruitment stages. Most previous studies discuss AI recruitment and human recruitment in a general way by focusing on their benefits, risks and ethical concerns. However, existing studies have provided limited explanation of how AI-based and human-based recruitment methods should be applied across specific recruitment stages, especially in determining which stages require higher AI involvement, higher human judgement or a combination of both.

Critical Structural Operational Gap: Without clear operational allocation matrices, organizations frequently face the twin dangers of algorithmic overuse in highly high-touch human interaction nodes (such as interviews and onboarding) leading to severe ethical breakdown and decreased candidate compliance, or extreme tech-underuse in data-heavy administrative nodes (such as high-volume screening and shortlisting) leading to massive corporate inefficiency.

1.6 Scope of Study

The scope of this study focuses on the role of AI-based recruitment methods and human-based recruitment methods across recruitment stages in Malaysia. This study investigates the level of AI and human involvement in six recruitment stages, which are identifying needs or job analysis, sourcing and attraction, screening and shortlisting, assessment, interview and induction and onboarding. These six recruitment stages are relevant to this study because they represent the main flow of recruitment activities in Malaysia, beginning from identifying job needs until the final stage of onboarding new employees.

Moreover, this study only focuses on identifying which recruitment stages are more suitable for AI-based recruitment methods or human-based recruitment methods and does not examine the specific AI tools, software platforms, or detailed data infrastructure architectures used. This restriction establishes an initial guide for corporate resource allocation rather than a purely technical programming manual. The target population consists exclusively of Malaysian corporate organizational practitioners operating inside an established, structured internal Human Resources Department. Fully outsourced HR frameworks are excluded since they lack direct operational management mapping oversight.

1.7 Significance of Study

Theoretical Level

Pushes the boundaries of automated Human Resource Management scholarship by offering explicit micro-level functional criteria mapping to reconcile systemic operational inefficiencies under the Human-AI Complementarity framework.

Practical Level

Provides local corporate decision-makers in Malaysia clear direction on where to deploy predictive machine models safely without causing algorithmic bias, loss of candidate trust, or corporate litigation.

Future Research

Establishes a rigorous stage-based empirical metric baseline for upcoming localized researchers exploring technological infrastructure upgrades across South East Asian labor markets.

1.8 Ethical Considerations

This study will be conducted in an ethical manner to protect the rights and privacy of the respondents. The first ethical consideration is informed consent and voluntary participation. In the questionnaire, Section A requires respondents to indicate whether they agree to participate in the study before answering the remaining questions. Respondents who do not agree to participate will be directed to the end of the questionnaire immediately.

Confidentiality and anonymity are protected by entirely excluding tracking variables (e.g., matching name, exact physical home address, or telephone logs). Sensitive, non-functional personal data like religious beliefs, race, or health conditions are omitted to prevent unconscious bias. Crucially, a multilingual survey system containing precise item-by-item translations in both English and Bahasa Malaysia is deployed. This structural inclusion completely removes English-language conversational barriers, preventing respondent discomfort while preventing systemic exclusion within Malaysia's diverse labor ecosystem.

2.0 Literature Review & Underpinning Theories

Sociotechnical Systems (STS) Theory

Originating from Trist and Bamforth’s (1951) classical studies, STS theory demonstrates that macro-organizational structural performance succeeds only when the social subsystem (human behavior, values, organizational culture) and the technical subsystem (algorithmic assets, database tools, computing systems) are optimally balanced. In this study, AI deployment across Malaysian firms cannot be viewed merely as a cold software upgrade; it is a sociotechnical shift that must respect local recruiter values and cultural nuances.

Human-AI Complementarity Theory

This theory evaluates systems based on specific capability asymmetries. It establishes that computational engines excel at high-volume data ingestion, mathematical pattern matching, and rapid automated filtering. Conversely, human agents retain complete cognitive dominance over complex contextual reasoning, ethical oversight, soft communication, and assessing cultural organizational fit.

3.0 Research Methodology

3.1 Research Design & Sampling

This study uses a quantitative, cross-sectional, comparative research design. Perceptions and actual behavior metrics will be collected from a targeted sample sizing minimum of 100 authenticated Human Resource practitioners, recruitment leads, and line hiring executives working across corporate Malaysia.

To manage localized corporate settings, a hybrid blend of purposive, convenience, and referral snowball sampling methods is engaged to successfully penetrate highly locked corporate HR networks.

3.3 Measurement Scaling Model

The physical research tool consists of five key sections (Consent, Screening, Demographics, Actual Metrics, and Recommended Metrics). Sections D and E employ a custom, balanced 5-point Likert Scale model ranging from 1 = Very Low/No Involvement up to 5 = Very High Involvement.

Rather than treating variables in isolation, the items are presented in a structural paired setup to gather immediate relational data between the target methods.

Planned Analytical Architecture Tools
